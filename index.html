I. DETERMINE WHAT INVENTION IS SOUGHT TO BE PATENTED

  • Obtaining prompt yet complete examination of patent applications is essential.
  • Under principles of compact prosecution, each claim should be reviewed for compliance with every statutory requirement for patentability
    • even if one or more claims are found deficient with respect to some statutory requirement.

Examiners

  • Examiners should:
    1. State all reasons and bases for rejecting claims in the first Office action.
    2. Explain deficiencies clearly, particularly when they serve as a basis for rejection.
    3. Indicate how rejections may be overcome and how problems may be solved, wherever practicable.
    4. State rejections that are not based on prior art (lack of adequate written description, enablement, or utility, etc.) with full development of the reasons rather than by mere conclusion.
    5. Failure to follow the above approach can lead to unnecessary delays in prosecution of the application.
  • Prior to focusing on specific statutory requirements, examiners must:
    • Determine exactly what the inventor has invented and is seeking to patent, and
    • How claims relate to and define that invention.
    • Examiners will:
      1. Review the complete specification, including:
        • Detailed description of the invention
        • Any specific embodiments disclosed
        • Any specific, substantial, and credible utilities asserted for the invention
        • Claims
      2. After obtaining an understanding of what the applicant invented:
        • Conduct a search of prior art
        • Determine if the invention as claimed complies with all statutory requirements

Additional Reading

  • MPEP Chapter 1400 covers examination of reissue applications.
  • MPEP Chapters 2200 (ex parte) and 2600 (inter partes) cover reexamination proceedings.
  • MPEP Chapter 2800 covers supplemental examination.

A. Identify and Understand Any Utility for the Invention

  • The claimed invention as a whole must be useful.
  • Purpose of this requirement:
    • Limit patent protection to inventions that possess a certain level of “real world” value.
    • Exclude subject matter that
      • represents nothing more than an idea or concept.
      • Or is simply a starting point for future investigation or research.

Case References:

  • Brenner v. Manson, 383 U.S. 519, 528-36, 148 USPQ 689, 693-96 (1966).
  • In re Fisher, 421 F.3d 1365, 76 USPQ2d 1225 (Fed. Cir. 2005).
  • In re Ziegler, 992 F.2d 1197, 1200-03, 26 USPQ2d 1600, 1603-06 (Fed. Cir. 1993).

Examiners

  • Examiners should review the application to identify any asserted utility.

Asserted Utility

  • The applicant or the inventor is in best position to explain why an invention is useful.
  • Accordingly, a complete disclosure should contain some indication of the practical application for the claimed invention.
    • i.e., why applicant asserts the claimed invention is useful.
  • Such a statement will usually explain
    • the purpose of the invention
    • or how the invention may be used.
    • e.g., a compound is believed to be useful in the treatment of a particular disorder.
  • Regardless of the form of statement of utility
    • it must enable one ordinarily skilled in the art to understand why the applicant asserts the claimed invention is useful.
  • Application may describe more than one utility and practical application, but only one is necessary.
  • Alternatively, an applicant may rely on the contemporaneous art to provide that the claimed invention has a well-established utility.

Practical Application

  • The concept of a “practical application” in the evaluation of utility
    • is different from the concept of whether a judicial exception is integrated into a “practical application” in the evaluation of subject matter eligibility.
    • See MPEP § 2106 for subject matter eligibility guidelines
    • See MPEP § 2107 for utility examination guidelines
